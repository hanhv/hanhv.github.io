1. Fourier Series of a \(2L\)-Periodic Function

Motivation: Why Fourier Series?

Many functions in science and engineering are periodic, meaning: \[ f(x + 2L) = f(x) \]

The key idea of Fourier series is: Any reasonable periodic function can be built from sine and cosine waves.

Fourier Series Formula

\[ f(x) \sim \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[ a_n \cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) + b_n \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \right] \]

This represents \( f(x) \) as an infinite sum of waves with different frequencies.

\[ a_0 = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\,dx \]
\[ a_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx \]
\[ b_n = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx \]

These integrals measure how much of each sine and cosine wave is present in \( f(x) \).

Interpretation

Each term in the Fourier series represents a wave:

  • Low \( n \): smooth, slow oscillations
  • High \( n \): rapid oscillations

Adding more terms improves the approximation of \( f(x) \).

Where Do These Formulas Come From?

The Fourier series formula for a \(2L\)-periodic function comes from two key ideas: periodicity and orthogonality of sine and cosine functions.

1. Why period \(2L\)?

If a function satisfies

\[ f(x + 2L) = f(x), \]

then its building blocks should also have the same period.

The functions

\[ \cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \]

have period \(2L\), because:

\[ \cos\left(\frac{n\pi(x+2L)}{L}\right) = \cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L} + 2n\pi\right) = \cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right). \]

So these are the natural “building blocks” for \(2L\)-periodic functions.

2. Orthogonality and Length of Sine and Cosine Functions (Key Property)

The functions \( \{ 1, \cos(\frac{n\pi x}{L}), \sin(\frac{n\pi x}{L}) \} \) form a pairwise orthogonal set on the interval \( [-L, L] \).

Orthogonality relations:

\[ \int_{-L}^{L} \cos\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \cos\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right) dx = 0, \quad n \ne m \]
\[ \int_{-L}^{L} \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \sin\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right) dx = 0, \quad n \ne m \]
\[ \int_{-L}^{L} \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \cos\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right) dx = 0 \]

Norms (lengths) of the functions:

\[ \int_{-L}^{L} \cos^2\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) dx = L, \quad \int_{-L}^{L} \sin^2\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) dx = L, \quad \int_{-L}^{L} 1^2 dx = 2L \]

3. Extracting Coefficients (Core Idea)

Multiply both sides of the Fourier series by \( \cos\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right) \) and integrate over \( [-L, L] \).

By orthogonality, almost all terms vanish, leaving:

\[ \int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\cos\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx = a_m \cdot L \]

So we obtain:

\[ a_m = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\cos\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx \]

Similarly:

\[ b_m = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\sin\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right)\,dx \]
\[ a_0 = \frac{1}{L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x)\,dx \]

Conclusion: The Fourier coefficients come from projecting \( f(x) \) onto sine and cosine functions, just like taking dot products in linear algebra.

2. Important Observations: Even and Odd Functions

If \( f(x) \) is even:

\[ b_n = 0 \]

If \( f(x) \) is odd:

\[ a_n = 0 \]

This simplifies calculations significantly.

3. Convergence Theorem

If \( f(x) \) is periodic and piecewise smooth, its Fourier series converges to:

  1. \( f(x) \) at points where \( f \) is continuous.
  2. and at a point of discontinuity \( x_0\):
    \[ \frac{f(x_0^+) + f(x_0^-)}{2}. \]

Example

Let \(f(t)\) be the \(2\pi\)-periodic function defined over one period by

\[ f(t) = \begin{cases} 0, & -\pi < t \le 0, \\ t, & 0 < t \le \pi. \end{cases} \]

Let \(\mathcal{F}(t)\) denote the Fourier series of \(f(t)\). Compute

\[ \mathcal{F}(0) + \mathcal{F}(\pi). \]

Solution:

We use the Fourier series convergence theorem.

Step 1: Evaluate at \(t=0\)

\(f\) is continuous at \(0\):

\[ f(0^-) = 0, \quad f(0^+) = 0 \implies \mathcal{F}(0) = f(0) = 0 \]

Step 2: Evaluate at \(t=\pi\)

\(f\) is discontinuous at \(\pi\) (jump):

\[ f(\pi^-) = \pi, \quad f(\pi^+) = f(-\pi^+) = 0 \implies \mathcal{F}(\pi) = \frac{\pi + 0}{2} = \frac{\pi}{2} \]

Step 3: Sum the values

\[ \mathcal{F}(0) + \mathcal{F}(\pi) = 0 + \frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi}{2} \]

Answer: div class="math-scroll"> \[ \mathcal{F}(0) + \mathcal{F}(\pi) = \frac{\pi}{2} \]

4. Use Fourier Series to Show Identities

Example

Let \( f(x) = |x| \) defined on \( (-\pi, \pi) \) and extended periodically with period \( 2\pi \). Use its Fourier series to compute the sum:

\[ \sum_{n=1,3,5,\dots}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}. \]

Step 1: Recognize function type

\(f(x) = |x|\) is even, so its Fourier series contains only cosines:

\[ f(x) \sim \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \cos(nx), \quad a_0 = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} |x| dx, \quad a_n = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} |x| \cos(nx) dx \]

Step 2: Compute \(a_0\)

\[ a_0 = \frac{1}{\pi} \left( \int_{-\pi}^{0} -x dx + \int_{0}^{\pi} x dx \right) = \frac{1}{\pi} (\frac{\pi^2}{2} + \frac{\pi^2}{2}) = \pi \]

Step 3: Compute \(a_n\)

Use symmetry: \(|x|\) is even, so double the integral from 0 to π:

\[ a_n = \frac{2}{\pi} \int_0^\pi x \cos(nx) dx \]

Integration by parts:

\[ \int_0^\pi x \cos(nx) dx = \frac{x \sin(nx)}{n} \Big|_0^\pi - \frac{1}{n} \int_0^\pi \sin(nx) dx = - \frac{1 - (-1)^n}{n^2} \]
\[ a_n = \frac{2}{\pi} \cdot \left(- \frac{1 - (-1)^n}{n^2}\right) = -\frac{2}{\pi} \cdot \frac{1 - (-1)^n}{n^2} \]

Step 4: Simplify for odd \(n\)

For odd \(n\), \((-1)^n = -1\), so:

\[ a_n = -\frac{4}{\pi n^2}, \quad n = 1,3,5,\dots \]

Step 5: Evaluate series at \(x = 0\)

Fourier series at \(x=0\):

\[ f(0) = 0 = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1,3,5,\dots} a_n \cos(0) = \frac{\pi}{2} + \sum_{n=1,3,5,\dots} \left(-\frac{4}{\pi n^2}\right) \]

Step 6: Solve for the sum

\[ 0 = \frac{\pi}{2} - \frac{4}{\pi} \sum_{n=1,3,5,\dots}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \sum_{n=1,3,5,\dots}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{8} \]

Answer:

\[ \boxed{\sum_{n=1,3,5,\dots}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{8}} \]
